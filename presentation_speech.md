# Presentation Speech — BFF Solution Design

---

## Full Script

My approach to this challenge comes from a unique perspective: **I build from the User experience backwards.** 

Coming from a strong Frontend background, I understand that a system is only as good as the experience it delivers to the end-user. This is why I am a firm believer in the **Backend-for-Frontend (BFF)** pattern. It acts as the critical "Bridge" between the fast-moving needs of the UI and the stable, complex world of core backend microservices. 

We have two very different portals here — a Customer Portal for direct users and an Agent Portal for professionals. A "generic" API would force the frontend to do too much heavy lifting. By using a BFF, we create a dedicated orchestration layer that handles the complexity of the backend while delivering a "User-First" API to the frontend.

---

With that "Bridge" mindset, my design process followed three steps:

**First**, I drafted a high-level architecture that maps the journey from the edge (WAF/API Gateway) down to the core microservices. I focused on how the BFF shields the frontend from the internal complexity of the Quotation and Payment services.

**Second**, I developed a sequence flow diagram. This surfaced a critical architectural insight: a policy purchase isn't just an API call; it’s a **Distributed Transaction**. Tracing this flow allowed me to design the "Safety Nets" needed—like the Saga pattern—to ensure that if a payment succeeds but a policy fails, the user is never left in limbo.

**Third**, I built a working POC. For me, the POC is where theory meets reality. It allowed me to physically validate the "Defensive Design" patterns I've proposed, such as Circuit Breakers for service outages and Idempotency locks to prevent accidental double-charges.

---

For **security and data integrity**, I've designed a "Defense-in-Depth" strategy:

At the "Front Door," **JWT** handles authentication. For **data in transit**, I've proposed **mTLS** to ensure service-to-service communication is encrypted and trusted. For **data at rest**, we use **Transparent Data Encryption** as a baseline, with **Application-Level Encryption (ALE)** for highly sensitive PII, like national identity numbers, ensuring they are encrypted before they even hit the database.

---

For **PCI compliance**, I’ve implemented a **Zero PCI Footprint** strategy. By tokenizing card data directly from the frontend to the payment gateway (like Stripe), our BFF and internal servers only ever handle opaque tokens. This is a business-driven design choice that reduces our audit scope from over 300 controls down to roughly 20, saving massive organizational risk and cost.

---

For **transactional integrity**, the BFF acts as the **Orchestrator of a Saga pattern**. 

Let me walk through the critical path. When a purchase request arrives, it carries an **Idempotency Key** generated by the frontend. The BFF first writes the pending order and a payment-initiated event atomically to its distributed state store. It then calls the Payment service. On success, it persists a payment-succeeded event and triggers Policy Binding. If that binding fails, the BFF automatically initiates a compensating transaction—a refund—by publishing a binding-failed event that triggers the reversal.

Now, there is a classic failure mode in distributed orchestrators: the **"Crash Gap."** What if the BFF dies after a successful payment but before persisting that fact? In my design, this gap is closed because every transition is written to an **Outbox Table** atomically with the state change. A reliable publisher processes that outbox. If the process crashes, the persisted event is picked up on restart. There is no window where a successful payment can be forgotten.

For the scenarios where the compensating transaction itself fails, I've designed a **Dead-Letter Queue (DLQ)** and a reconciliation job. This is the final safety net. It ensures that any failed refund is eventually detected, alerted, and resolved. No orphaned charge is ever left behind.

---

For **resilience**, the BFF implements **Circuit Breakers** on all downstream REST calls. If a service like the Quotation MS fails repeatedly, the circuit trips to an OPEN state, failing-fast to protect the system from cascading outages. By combining this with the **Idempotency Keys** I mentioned earlier, we ensure that even if the network is flaky, the customer is only ever charged once.

---

For **observability**, I've used my "User-First" lens to implement **Distributed Tracing**. Every request generates an **X-Correlation-Id** that follows the request from the UI, through the BFF, and into the microservices. This allows our operations teams to find and fix any production issue in seconds, rather than hours. All logs are also automatically **scrubbed of PII** before ingestion to maintain strict compliance.

---

Finally, for **scalability**, the BFFs are fully stateless and managed by **Kubernetes**. This allows us to scale the Customer BFF independently of the Agent BFF using **Horizontal Pod Autoscaling**, ensuring we only pay for the resources we are actually using.

---

**In conclusion:**

What I am presenting today is more than just a backend design. It is a **Resilient Bridge**. 

By combining my Frontend understanding of the user's needs with "Defensive" backend patterns like Sagas, Idempotency, and Circuit Breakers, we've created a platform that is secure, compliant, and—most importantly—built to survive the realities of a distributed network. It’s a design that a compliance officer, an engineer, and a customer can all trust.
